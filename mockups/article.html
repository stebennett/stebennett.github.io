You Don't Need a Bug Tracker

14 October 2024 • 8 min read • 1,542 words

I've been thinking a lot about how engineering teams track bugs, and I've come to a conclusion that might seem controversial: most teams don't actually need a dedicated bug tracker. What they need is a better process.

The Problem with Bug Trackers

Bug trackers are where bugs go to die. Every team I've worked with has accumulated a growing backlog of bugs that nobody ever looks at. The list grows and grows, becoming a source of guilt rather than a useful tool.

Consider this: if you have 200 open bugs in your tracker, how many of them are still relevant? How many are duplicates? How many have already been fixed by other changes? In my experience, fewer than 20% of a stale bug backlog contains actionable items.

The best bug tracking system is one where bugs don't accumulate in the first place.

An Alternative Approach

Instead of tracking every bug in a dedicated system, consider this approach:

  1. Fix it now — If a bug is reported and it's small, fix it immediately. Don't create a ticket. Don't add it to a sprint. Just fix it.
  2. Fix it next — If it's too large to fix immediately, make it the next thing you work on. No tracker needed.
  3. Accept it — If it's not worth fixing now or next, it's probably not worth tracking either. Accept it as a known limitation.

This is a simplified version of the approach, but the principle holds: reduce the distance between discovery and resolution.

What This Looks Like in Practice

When a team member finds a bug, the conversation might look something like this in your #engineering Slack channel:

@sarah: Found a bug - the date picker doesn't handle timezones
         correctly when the user is in UTC+12

@tom:    That's in the DateSelector component, right? I can fix
         that this afternoon. It's a one-liner.

@sarah:  Perfect, thanks! I'll verify the fix when it's deployed.

No ticket created. No priority assigned. No sprint planning required. The bug was found, communicated, and fixed within hours.

When You Do Need a Bug Tracker

There are situations where bug tracking makes sense:

  • Regulatory or compliance requirements demand an audit trail
  • You're building a product where external customers report bugs through a formal channel
  • Your team is distributed across many timezones and async communication is the norm

But even in these cases, the goal should be to minimise the time a bug spends in the tracker. Track it, yes — but don't let it sit there for months.

The Metrics That Matter

If you do use a bug tracker, the most important metric isn't how many bugs you've logged — it's how quickly you resolve them. A team that finds and fixes 10 bugs a week with a median resolution time of 2 hours is far healthier than a team that logs 50 bugs a week and resolves them in 3 months.

Here's a simple dashboard you might use:

Metric Target Current
Median time to resolution < 4 hours 2.3 hours
Open bugs older than 7 days 0 3
Bugs resolved this week — 12

Conclusion

The best teams I've worked with don't have elaborate bug tracking processes. They have a culture of ownership, where finding a bug means fixing a bug. The tracker, if it exists at all, is a thin layer — not a bureaucratic bottleneck.

Start by asking your team: how many of our open bugs are actually going to get fixed? If the honest answer is "not many", it might be time to rethink your approach.
